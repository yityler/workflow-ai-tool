DESIGN KNOWLEDGE BASE
Curated from the teachings of human designers and design research. Used as the RAG source for
the storefront generator: the relevant chunks below are retrieved and given to the AI at design
time, so the prompt itself only carries company-specific information.

TOPIC: TIMELESS DESIGN PRINCIPLES (Dieter Rams, industrial designer, Braun)
Good design is as little design as possible: less, but better, because it concentrates on the
essential aspects. Good design is unobtrusive — products are like tools, neutral and restrained.
Good design is honest: it does not make a product seem more valuable than it is. Good design is
thorough down to the last detail: nothing arbitrary, nothing left to chance; care and accuracy
show respect for the user. Good design makes a product understandable — at best, it makes the
product talk. For a screen: remove every element that does not help the customer act, and make
the remaining elements explain themselves.

TOPIC: THE FOUR BASIC PRINCIPLES — CONTRAST, REPETITION, ALIGNMENT, PROXIMITY (Robin Williams,
The Non-Designer's Design Book)
Contrast: if two elements are not the same, make them very different — timid differences read as
mistakes. Repetition: repeat visual treatments (colors, weights, spacing, corner radii) through
the whole design to unify it. Alignment: nothing should be placed arbitrarily; every element
should have a visual connection to another element — pick one strong alignment edge and keep it.
Proximity: group related items close together so they read as one unit; separate unrelated items
with real space. Most amateur designs fail on proximity and alignment before anything else.

TOPIC: TYPOGRAPHY DISCIPLINE (Massimo Vignelli, the Vignelli Canon)
Use very few typefaces — one or two, chosen well, used with discipline. Type size changes should
be meaningful jumps, not timid steps. The grid is the underwear of design: align text to a
consistent structure. White space is not empty space; it is what gives typography its dignity.
For a kiosk screen: one type family, three sizes maximum, weights doing the hierarchy work.

TOPIC: COLOR HARMONY AND THE COLOR WHEEL (Johannes Itten, Bauhaus)
Reliable palette recipes from the color wheel: monochromatic (tints and shades of one hue) is
cohesive and almost impossible to get wrong — the safest scheme for a branded interface, derive
light tints of the brand hue for backgrounds and accents. Analogous (adjacent hues) feels calm
and unified. Complementary (opposite hues) creates maximum tension — use only for the one element
that must grab attention, never for large areas. In practice for UI: build the interface almost
entirely from neutrals plus tints of the single brand hue; introduce a second hue only with a
clear job to do.

TOPIC: COLOR PROPORTION AND RESTRAINT (60-30-10 rule; Refactoring UI, Adam Wathan and Steve
Schoger)
Distribute color 60-30-10: about 60% neutral background, 30% surfaces and secondary elements,
10% accent. The accent color is a budget — spend it only on the price, the primary action, and
at most one highlight. Greys should not be pure grey: tint them slightly toward the brand hue
(cool blue-greys for cool brands, warm stone-greys for warm brands). Text hierarchy needs only
three colors: near-black for primary, medium grey for secondary, light grey for tertiary. Never
use pure black on pure white; soften both slightly. Always keep text contrast accessible: dark
text on light backgrounds, light text on dark accents.

TOPIC: COLOR PSYCHOLOGY IN RETAIL
Blue reads as trust, security, and calm — the most common choice for payment moments. Green reads
as freshness, health, and go/confirm. Red stimulates urgency and action but raises anxiety at
payment; use sparingly near totals. Black and charcoal read premium and minimal. Warm neutrals
(cream, stone) read friendly and boutique; cool neutrals read modern and technical. Whatever the
brand color, do not fight it: derive the mood from the brand hue rather than introducing a
clashing scheme at checkout, where trust matters most.

TOPIC: TYPOGRAPHIC HIERARCHY AND READABILITY
Use a deliberate type scale with meaningful jumps (for example 13, 15, 20, 26, 32) — adjacent
sizes must differ enough to signal different levels. Prefer changing weight (600-700 versus 400)
over adding more sizes. Body text line-height around 1.5; large headings tighter, around 1.2.
Keep line length between 45 and 75 characters. Align mixed-size text on the baseline. Sans-serif
projects clean, modern, honest — the default for screens; serif projects tradition and authority.
Labels: remove them when the value is self-evident ("Jane Doe" not "Name: Jane Doe"); combine
label and value into one phrase ("12 left in stock"). Right-align numbers so they compare easily.

TOPIC: SPACING, LAYOUT, AND WHITESPACE
Design on an 8-point grid: all padding, margins, and gaps in multiples of 8 (8, 16, 24, 32, 48).
Start with too much whitespace and remove until it feels right — cramped is the most common
amateur mistake. Always more space BETWEEN groups than WITHIN them, or grouping becomes
ambiguous. Constrain content width rather than filling the screen edge to edge. Use soft
two-layer shadows to separate surfaces instead of borders; use background-color changes and
spacing before reaching for lines. Consistent corner radii everywhere — pick one radius family.

TOPIC: VISUAL HIERARCHY AND FOCAL POINT
Every screen needs exactly one focal point — decide what the customer must see first and make it
unmissable through size, color, and position; then actively de-emphasize everything else rather
than emphasizing more things. Users scan in an F or Z pattern from the top left: put the message
at the top, the action at the natural end point. A visually pleasing design uses no more than
three sizes of anything. If everything is bold, nothing is bold.

TOPIC: PRODUCT PAGE DESIGN FOR RETAIL
The product presentation (image or visual tile) and the name carry the page; give them room. Show
the price near the product identity, large and unambiguous, with tax context in small muted text
beside it — never hide costs (hidden costs are the top reason buyers abandon). One primary
call-to-action per page, styled as the single loudest element. Supporting details (SKU, category,
description) are secondary: smaller, muted, never competing with name, price, or action.

TOPIC: CHECKOUT AND PAYMENT UX (Baymard Institute research)
Checkout succeeds through perceived simplicity: show a clear itemized order summary, keep the
arithmetic (subtotal, tax) small and muted, and make the final total the single dominant number
on the screen. Exactly one prominent call-to-action ("Pay now") with nothing competing near it.
A clean, professional, bug-free appearance directly increases trust — people judge security by
polish. Remove every distraction from the payment moment; reassure with a short line about help
or safety near the action. Show progress or completion cues — people accelerate as they near the
end (goal-gradient effect), and remember the ending most (peak-end rule).

TOPIC: KIOSK AND TOUCHSCREEN ERGONOMICS
Design for a standing customer at arm's length who decides in about three seconds. Touch targets
minimum 48 pixels tall with clear spacing between tappable things; the primary button should be
the biggest, easiest target on screen (Fitts's law). Body text noticeably larger than on desktop
web. Show at most five to six content blocks per screen (Miller's law); fewer choices mean faster
decisions (Hick's law). Follow familiar retail patterns rather than inventing novel layouts
(Jakob's law). Plain language, icons with labels, help always one obvious tap away.

TOPIC: MICROCOPY AND VOICE FOR SELF-SERVICE RETAIL
Voice: warm, confident, plain. Short sentences. No jargon, no technical terms, at most one
exclamation mark per screen. Headlines reassure and instruct in five words or fewer ("Just place
your items down"). Calls-to-action start with a verb and stay under three words ("Pay now",
"Start scanning"). Use reassurance where anxiety happens: near payment, near totals, near
anything automatic ("Everything detected automatically"). Highlight at most ONE offer — a
highlight is only memorable when it is rare (Von Restorff effect). Attractive, polished screens
are perceived as easier to use and more trustworthy (aesthetic-usability effect).

TOPIC: AVOIDING THE GENERIC AI-GENERATED LOOK
Viewers can instantly recognize machine-generated design by its tells: identical padding,
identical corner radius, and identical card heights everywhere; every section centered; flat
visual rhythm where each band gets equal weight; default indigo-purple gradients; and vague
aspirational copy. Human-made design shows intention: alignment varies purposefully (a
left-aligned hero reads more editorial than a centered one), spacing breathes differently
between sections, ONE element per screen gets a memorable treatment while the rest stay quiet,
and every color has a job rather than being decoration. The single strongest fix is specificity:
real product names, real numbers, real details of THIS store — averaged, could-be-anyone design
is what reads as machine-made.

TOPIC: BUILDING A FULL PALETTE FROM ONE BRAND COLOR (HSL tonal scales)
Professional palettes are derived, not picked: keep the brand color's HUE constant and move
lightness and saturation to build a tonal scale from near-white (steps 50-100) to near-black
(steps 900-950). Tints: raise lightness toward 97% and cut saturation by 30-70% — these become
backgrounds and hover states. Shades: lower lightness and reduce saturation slightly (15-25%)
— these become text and emphasis. Crucially, even the "greys" should carry a whisper of the
brand hue: hue-tinted neutrals are what make a page feel art-directed instead of assembled.
Never use pure black text or pure grey surfaces; tint everything gently toward the brand.

TOPIC: SPECIFICITY AND VOICE — COPY THAT SOUNDS HUMAN
Generic AI copy hedges and inflates: "effortless", "seamless", "elevate", "unlock", "world-class".
Human copy commits to specifics: name the actual thing, the actual number, the actual moment
("Set your basket down — every tag reads in one second" beats "Experience seamless checkout").
Write in one consistent voice from header to footer. Prefer verbs and nouns over adjectives.
One idea per line. If a sentence could appear on any store's website, it is not finished —
rewrite it with a detail only this store could claim.

TOPIC: RHYTHM, ASYMMETRY, AND THE ONE MEMORABLE MOMENT
Uniformity reads as machine-made; rhythm reads as designed. Vary section spacing deliberately:
let the hero breathe with generous padding, keep utility rows compact. Mix alignments with
intent — hero text left-aligned with an off-center visual creates editorial energy; reserve
centering for single focal moments like a total or confirmation. Give each screen exactly one
memorable treatment (an oversized number, a bold accent band, an unexpected crop) and keep
everything else restrained so the moment lands. Slightly varied corner radii and asymmetric
column widths (a 60/40 split instead of 50/50) are quiet signals of human intention.

TOPIC: WRITING FOR THE WEB — HOW PEOPLE ACTUALLY READ (Nielsen Norman eyetracking research)
People do not read screens; they scan them. 79% of users scan every new page; they read at most
20-28% of the words, in an F-shaped pattern: across the top, a shorter sweep below, then down the
left edge. Therefore: front-load — the first two words of every heading, sentence, and bullet
must carry the information, because those are the words the scanning eye actually lands on. Put
the conclusion first, details after. Write half the words you would write on paper, then cut
half again. One idea per paragraph. Use meaningful subheadings and bullets so the left edge of
the text tells the whole story. Write numbers as numerals (9%, 3 items) — numerals stop the
scanning eye, spelled-out numbers do not. High-quality writing is what converts a scanner into
a reader.

TOPIC: WHAT NOT TO WRITE — HAPPY TALK AND NEEDLESS WORDS (Steve Krug, Don't Make Me Think)
Happy talk must die: content-free welcome text ("Welcome to our store! We're so glad you're
here...") is small talk that costs attention and delivers nothing — customers want the point.
If a sentence only exists to be sociable, delete it. Omit needless words and empty modifiers:
"very", "really", "world-class", "state-of-the-art" add length, not meaning. Nobody reads
instructions — design things to be self-explanatory; when guidance is unavoidable, make it
brief, timely, and impossible to miss. Prefer active voice ("We detected 3 items" not "3 items
have been detected"). Prefer positive phrasing over negative. Clear always beats clever: a plain
label that works outranks a witty one that makes someone think. Buttons must name the action
that happens next, starting with a verb ("Pay now", "View basket") — never "Click here",
"Submit", or "OK".

TOPIC: TYPEFACE PERSONALITY — MATCHING THE FONT TO THE BUSINESS
A typeface is the brand's voice made visible; choose it from the business, not from habit.
Serif faces (Playfair Display, Lora, Cormorant) read as heritage, luxury, authority, and
tradition — right for premium, financial, and established brands; research links serifs to
higher perceived trustworthiness. Clean geometric sans-serifs (Space Grotesk, Work Sans,
Futura-likes) read as modern, technological, efficient — right for tech-forward and minimal
brands. Rounded sans-serifs (Nunito, Quicksand) read as warm, friendly, approachable — right
for family, community, and everyday retail brands. Condensed display faces (Bebas Neue) read
as loud, confident, fashionable — right for bold youth-oriented brands, used for headlines
only. Script and handwritten faces (Dancing Script, Pacifico) read as artisanal, personal,
and crafted by hand — right for candle shops, bakeries, florists, and boutique makers;
keep script to headings only and pair it with a plain sans body for legibility. Match the
trade, not a generic mood: a candle store wants script warmth, a sports-equipment store
wants a bold condensed display, a bank or pharmacy wants a trustworthy serif, a gadget
brand wants a clean geometric sans. Pairing rule: contrast without conflict — one distinctive display face for headings,
one neutral workhorse for body text, and never more than two families. Using the default
system font everywhere is a tell of machine-generated design; a deliberate display face is
the fastest way to look art-directed.
